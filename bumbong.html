Using ancient steaming methods and native bamboo tubes (bumbong), early Filipinos created steamed rice cakes that were labor-intensive but highly treasured during harvest festivals and rituals. The Sensory Magic of Puto Bumbong

Puto Bumbong is steamed in bamboo tubes that sit vertically over boiling water, creating a soft, chewy texture with a faint smoky scent from the banana leaves. The toppings are everything‚Äîbutter or margarine for richness, muscovado sugar or brown sugar for depth, and grated coconut for freshness. Each bite is a harmony of textures and flavors: warm, sweet, nutty, and lightly salted from the butter. It's nostalgia wrapped in banana leaves.

Recipe & Ingredients

Ingredients

  • 1 cup glutinous rice flour
  • 1/4 cup regular rice flour
  • 1/2 cup water (adjust as needed)
  • A few drops of ube or purple food coloring (if pirurutong is unavailable)
  • Banana leaves, lightly heated for flexibility
  • Melted butter or margarine
  • Grated coconut
  • Brown sugar or muscovado sugar

Instructions

  1. In a bowl, mix glutinous rice flour and rice flour.
  2. Slowly add water until a sticky dough forms. If using food coloring, mix it in now.
  3. Roll small portions into logs (about finger-length).
  4. Steam for 8‚Äì10 minutes, ideally using bamboo tubes or a regular steamer lined with banana leaves for aroma.
  5. Lay steamed puto bumbong logs on pre-cut banana leaves.
  6. Brush generously with melted butter, sprinkle with brown sugar, and top with grated coconut.
  7. Serve warm with hot salabat (ginger tea) or tsokolate (Filipino hot chocolate).
