A Rooted History: From Survival Crop to Celebration Treat

Cassava was introduced to the Philippines during the Spanish colonial period via the Manila-Acapulco Galleon Trade from Latin America. It quickly became a staple among rural communities due to its ability to grow in poor soil and feed families even during hard times. In the provinces, especially in Visayas and Mindanao, cassava was not just food‚Äîit was survival. But leave it to the Filipino spirit to turn a survival crop into something extraordinary. Cassava Cake is believed to have evolved from simple steamed cassava into the baked dessert we know today, thanks to the introduction of dairy products like condensed milk and cheese. What was once a basic rural snack became a festive treat served in birthdays, fiestas, and holiday gatherings.

Recipe & Ingredients

Ingredients

  • 2 cups grated cassava (fresh or frozen)
  • 1 cup grated coconut (or 1 cup coconut milk)
  • 1 can (300 ml) condensed milk
  • 1 can (370 ml) evaporated milk
  • 2 eggs
  • 1/2 cup sugar
  • 1/4 cup melted butter
  • Optional: macapuno strips or grated cheese

For the topping:

  • 1/2 cup condensed milk
  • 1/2 cup evaporated milk
  • 1 egg
  • Optional: grated cheese for topping

Instructions

  1. In a large mixing bowl, combine grated cassava, coconut, condensed milk, evaporated milk, eggs, sugar, and melted butter.
  2. Mix until well blended. Add macapuno if desired.
  3. Pour the mixture into a greased baking dish (8x8 inch works well).
  4. Bake at 175¬∞C (350¬∞F) for 45‚Äì50 minutes, or until set and lightly golden on top.
  5. While baking, mix the topping ingredients in a bowl.
  6. Remove cake from oven, pour the topping mixture over it, and return to oven.
  7. Bake for another 15‚Äì20 minutes, or until the top is set and slightly browned.
  8. Optional: Broil for 2‚Äì3 minutes to get a caramelized top.
  9. Let cool slightly before slicing.
  10. Best served warm‚Äîor chilled for a firmer bite!
